What is Misinformation?

Misinformation is false or inaccurate information. Examples of misinformation include false rumours, or insults and pranks, while examples of more deliberate disinformation include malicious content such as hoaxes, spear phishing and computational propaganda. The terms "misinformation" and "disinformation" have often been associated with the neologism "fake news", defined by some scholars as "fabricated information that mimics news media content in form but not in organizational process or intent"

Misinformation dring COVID-19 Pandemic

Outbreak of the COVID-19 Pandemic has also lead to a spiralling ‘infodemic’ crisis due to widespread spread of misinformation and disinformation. Infodemic is an overabundance of information and the rapid spread of misleading or fabricated news, images, and videos. Like the virus, it is highly contagious and grows exponentially. It also complicates COVID-19 pandemic response efforts. Proliferating misinformation — even when the content is, in a best-case scenario, harmless — can have serious and even social and lethal health ramifications in the context of a global pandemic. Even as the world is laser-focused on the search for a safe, effective vaccine, misinformation continues to spread about immunization as well.

Incidents due to Misinformation during COVID-19

The infodemic has lead to numerous incidents across the world, effecting the economies of the countries hugely. In some countries, rumours about impending food scarcity prompted people to stockpile supplies early on in the epidemic and caused actual shortages.

In the United States of America, a person passed away from ingesting a fish tank cleaning product containing chloroquine after reports mentioned hydroxychloroquine as a possible - yet unproven - remedy for treatment of COVID-19. In the Islamic Republic of Iran, over three hundred people died after drinking methanol alcohol that social media messages said had cured others of the coronavirus.

“Plandemic” a 26-minute conspiracy theory video falsely accuses Dr Anthony Fauci, the leading infectious disease expert in the United States, of manufacturing the virus and sending it to China. The same video falsely claims that wearing masks will lead to self-infection. More than 8 million people watched the video across social media before it was taken down.

Why is Misinformation spread?

People often spread misinformation for social motivated reasons and for manipulating the public. This might me done to defame a person for political or social reasons. Because of the decentralized nature and structure of the Internet, writers can easily publish content without being required to subject it to peer review, prove their qualifications, or provide backup documentation.
